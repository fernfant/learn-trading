Lesson 11 · 🏆 Capstone — beat buy-and-hold, after costs

The boss fight. Everything from Track 1 in one honest backtest: a random-walk price, an MA-crossover signal, risk-sized positions, stops, leverage — versus simply buying and holding. The catch: it must win after the spread, overnight funding and slippage, and it's judged on out-of-sample data it never tuned on. The honest truth? On a near-random market, after costs, that's rare.

Why the verdict keeps flipping. The market is a random walk. On any single split the strategy might win or lose — often it "wins" only by sitting flat through a drop while buy-and-hold bleeds. That's risk avoidance, not prediction. Hit Test 20 markets: a real edge beats B&H across most of them. This one rarely does — that's the honest result, not a bug.
💡 Toggle costs off and watch the gap shrink. Much of what separates the strategy from buy-and-hold is simply the spread it pays on every crossing plus the swap on every held day. Certain costs eating an uncertain edge is precisely what "most retail CFD accounts lose money" means.
🏆 You finished Track 1 — you can now say…
  • An honest backtest is net of all costs, split into in-sample (tune) and out-of-sample (judge), with no peeking.
  • The number that counts is the out-of-sample one — the in-sample result always flatters you because the knobs were fit to it.
  • Robustness across many markets > one lucky split. A coin-flip win rate means no edge.
  • On a near-random walk, after spread + swap + slippage, durably beating buy-and-hold is genuinely rare — the whole theme of Track 1.
  • You built a real backtester one line per lesson: price → spread → long/short → equity → leverage → orders → sizing → signal → costs → metrics → capstone.
