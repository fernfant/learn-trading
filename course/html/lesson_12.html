Lesson 12 · From price-taker to price-maker

The hinge of the whole course. In Track 1 you were the customer — a price-taker. You crossed the spread and paid it on every trade. Now flip seats. Someone quoted you those two prices and pocketed the gap — a market maker, a price-maker (on capital.com, the broker). Same number, opposite sign: cost → revenue. No code this lesson — just the seat change.

🔁 Flip the seat: one round trip, two ledgers

A customer gets in (buys at the ask) and out (sells at the bid) — they lose the spread. The market maker takes the opposite side of both legs and books the spread as profit. Pick a seat, then play the trade and watch the same 0.04 spread flow the other way.

YOU: CUSTOMER · price-TAKER
open: buy @ ask—
close: sell @ bid—
$0.00
spread = ask − bid = 100.02 − 99.98 = $0.04
Pick a seat and play the round trip.
ask (sell price you're quoted) bid (buy price you're quoted)
💡 It's the exact same four cents. The customer's ledger shows it in red (a cost); the maker's shows it in green (revenue). Nobody predicted which way the price went — the maker simply stood in the middle and got paid a fee for providing liquidity. That fee is the spread.
The maker is not printing free money. Each fill leaves the maker holding a position they didn't want — that's inventory risk (Lesson 19) — and some takers know something you don't: the informed trader from Lesson 0, your faint signal in Track 1, is now your hazard. When they pick you off it's called adverse selection (Lesson 21). Staying profitable and flat is the actual job — the Lesson 22 capstone.
✅ You can now say…
  • A price-taker crosses the spread and pays it; a price-maker quotes both sides and earns it.
  • It's the same spread seen from opposite seats — your cost in Track 1 is the maker's revenue in Track 2.
  • On capital.com the broker is the market maker; Track 2 builds that machine in ../exchange.py.
  • Earning the spread isn't free: inventory risk and adverse selection are the maker's hazards.
