Lesson 16 · The matching engine loop — crossed orders become trades

This is the heart of every exchange. When an incoming order crosses the book — a buy priced at or above the best ask, or a sell at or below the best bid — the engine matches them into a trade, printed at the resting order's price. It eats size from both, then loops while the order keeps crossing. Whatever's left over rests as a new limit. One loop, run on every order, is the machine.

⚙️ Submit an order into a live book

Pick a side, a price, and a size, then Submit. If it crosses, watch it sweep the best opposite levels — each fill prints to the tape — and any leftover drops in as a new resting level. If it doesn't cross, it just rests. Bids are green, asks are red.

💡 Trades print at the resting price, not yours. The order already in the book is the maker — it set the price and waited (Lesson 15's price-time priority). Your incoming order is the taker, crossing the spread to hit it. The maker sets the terms; the taker pays them. That's the Lesson 12 flip, made literal.
Crossed → trade, else rest. That single test runs on every order. A market order (Lesson 14) is just a price so aggressive it always crosses. A non-crossing limit (Lesson 15) skips the loop and rests. One machine, both behaviours — try a buy at 99.50 (rests) versus a buy at 101.00 (sweeps everything).
✅ You can now say…
  • An order crosses when a buy's price ≥ the best ask, or a sell's price ≤ the best bid.
  • A match trades the smaller size at the resting order's price, then loops while it keeps crossing.
  • Any leftover rests as a new limit; a non-crossing order rests immediately.
  • This loop — the match() core of exchange.py — is the heart of an exchange.
