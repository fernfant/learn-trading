Lesson 8 · A signal: the moving-average crossover

A moving average is just the mean of the last few prices — it smooths the noise. The famous rule: when the fast average crosses above the slow one, go long; when it crosses back below, go flat. It looks like wisdom. Below you can test it — and watch it flip from hero to zero as you reseed the market.

Most "signals" are noise. On a near-random price the crossover trades on wiggles, not insight — sometimes it wins, sometimes it loses, and a tiny per-trade cost drags it below buy & hold (which pays the toll only once). Hit reseed a dozen times: a rule with a real edge would win consistently. This one doesn't.
💡 The honest test has two more parts. An edge must survive costs (Lesson 9 — spread, overnight funding, slippage) and out-of-sample data you didn't tune on (Lesson 10 — return, Sharpe, drawdown). Beating one cherry-picked chart proves nothing.
✅ You can now say…
  • A moving average is the mean of the last N prices; a fast one reacts quickly, a slow one lags.
  • The crossover rule is one line: signal = fast_ma > slow_ma → long; flip back → flat.
  • On a near-random walk it mostly trades noise — it doesn't reliably beat buy & hold or even a coin flip.
  • It crosses the spread on every trade, so a small cost can sink it; buy & hold pays once.
  • A claimed edge must survive costs and out-of-sample data — the teaser for L9 and L10.
