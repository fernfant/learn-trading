Lesson 0 · Foundations — what are we even trading?

The only lesson with no code. Before you bet a cent, three questions: what is this thing, where does its price come from, and what game am I playing? An asset has a value (what it's worth) and a price (the last number two people traded at) — and those are not the same thing. The whole course lives in that gap.

⚖️ Where a price comes from: supply meets demand

Nobody announces the price — it emerges. Buyers post the most they'll pay (demand); sellers post the least they'll accept (supply). The price is where the two curves cross. Add eager buyers or sellers and watch it move.

More buyers than sellers pushes the price up. That tug-of-war is the price.
💡 That list of every bid and ask has a name: the order book. In Track 2 you'll build it (Lesson 13). The single price you trade against in Track 1 is just the top of a book someone else is running.

🎯 Value vs price: two different jobs

The dashed gold line is the asset's true value. The solid line is its price, which wanders. Flip informed traders on and watch the price get tugged back toward value; flip them off and it drifts off on its own. Push the value with news.

🏦 Investor watches the gap: buy when price < value, wait. Trader rides the wiggle: which way next, after costs? 📈
This is a trading course, on purpose. Over a short horizon, price moves are dominated by who's buying and selling right now — not by slow changes in value. And on capital.com you trade CFDs: leveraged, two-directional bets on the price difference, not ownership of the asset. So we set deep valuation aside — a deliberate, explained choice, not a gap.
🔁 The informed trader shows up twice. In Track 1 they're the faint signal you hunt — the reason markets aren't perfectly random. In Track 2, once you're the market maker quoting prices, those same informed traders pick you off — a hazard with a name: adverse selection (Lesson 21). Same people, opposite seat.
✅ You can now say…
  • An asset has a value (what it's worth) and a price (the last traded number) — not the same thing.
  • A price emerges from buyers' bids and sellers' asks meeting — supply vs demand — which is the order book.
  • Investing asks what is it worth?; trading asks which way does the price move? This course is firmly trading.
  • A CFD is a leveraged, two-way bet on price difference, not ownership — which is why valuation is out of scope.
  • Markets are nearly random; the small real edges come from informed traders — Track 1's signal, Track 2's hazard.
