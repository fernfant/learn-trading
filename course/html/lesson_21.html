Lesson 21 · Adverse selection — toxic flow runs you over

You're the market maker. You quote a bid below fair and an ask above it, hoping to get hit on both sides and bank the spread (Lesson 18). But who trades with you decides everything. Noise flow is harmless — it pays the spread and predicts nothing. Informed flow is toxic: it trades with you right before the price moves against you. You earn small spreads from the many and bleed big losses to the few.

🩸 Run the flow and watch the toxic fills bleed you

Drag informed flow up to make more takers toxic. Each noise fill ticks your equity up a little; each informed fill flashes red and knocks it down a lot. Then fight back: widen your half-spread to charge everyone more.

You can't tell a shark from a minnow in advance. A toxic order looks exactly like a harmless one — someone lifting your ask or hitting your bid. The damage only shows up after they trade, when the price jumps your way. That is the cruelty of adverse selection (Glosten–Milgrom; Kyle): the very wish to trade with you is mild evidence that the trade is bad for you.
💡 Widening is a tax on the innocent. Drag the half-spread up and watch the green noise bar grow far more than the red informed bar shrinks. You can't deter the sharks, so you charge the harmless majority more to cover the toxic minority. The catch (next lesson): a wider quote sits further from fair and wins less flow. Too tight and toxic flow eats you; too wide and you trade with nobody.
🔁 The informed trader, both seats. Back in Lesson 0 the informed trader was the faint signal you hunted in Track 1 — the reason a price isn't a perfect coin flip. Now, quoting prices in Track 2, that same person is the hazard that bleeds you. Same trader, opposite seat. That symmetry is the whole course in one idea.
✅ You can now say…
  • The market maker's edge is the spread, earned from harmless noise flow.
  • Informed (toxic) flow trades with you right before the price moves against you — you're left on the wrong side.
  • Adverse selection: the wish to trade with you is evidence the trade is bad for you (Glosten–Milgrom, Kyle).
  • Raise the informed fraction and the same bot's P&L falls and can go negative.
  • The blunt defense is to widen the spread — taxing the noise majority to cover the toxic minority.
  • Track 1's signal is Track 2's hazard — same informed trader, opposite seat.
