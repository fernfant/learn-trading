Lesson 14 · A market order eats the book

A market order names no price. It says "fill me NOW, whatever it costs." It consumes the book from the best price outward — a small order is swallowed at the top, but a big order walks the book and pays a worse average price than the top. That gap is slippage — the very cost you paid as a customer in Track 1, now seen from the inside.

🍽️ Send a market order and watch it eat levels

Pick a side and a size, then Send. A market BUY eats the cheapest asks first; a SELL hits the highest bids first. Consumed size turns gold. Watch the average fill (VWAP) drift away from the top of book as you walk deeper.

A small order fills at the top — no slippage. Drag the size up.
💡 VWAP is the price you actually paid. The number at the top of the book is only good for the size resting there. The moment your order is bigger than the top level, you reach up (buys) or down (sells) for worse prices, and your volume-weighted average price drifts away from it.
A market order trades price for certainty. It always fills — until the book runs dry — but you don't control the price. Bigger order ⇒ walks deeper ⇒ more slippage. Thinner book ⇒ same. This is the Track 1 slippage from Lesson 9, now explained mechanically.
✅ You can now say…
  • A market order fills now at any price, eating the book from the best level outward.
  • A small order fills at the top; a big one walks the book to worse levels.
  • VWAP (volume-weighted average price) is the real price paid; slippage = VWAP vs top of book.
  • The line you added to exchange.py: match_market(side, size), walking the levels.
  • Bigger order or thinner book ⇒ more slippage. Liquidity near the top is what protects you.
