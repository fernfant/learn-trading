Lesson 10 · Backtesting honestly — return, Sharpe & drawdown

You have a strategy. The last question before you risk a cent: is it any good — and how would I know? Three numbers describe almost any strategy, all read off the equity curve: total return (the headline), Sharpe (return per unit of wobble), and max drawdown (the worst peak-to-trough fall — the pain). Then the trap that kills most backtests: tuning on the past (in-sample) and mistaking it for skill on the future (out-of-sample).

This is overfitting. The best in-sample setting almost never wins out-of-sample — on a near-random price path (Lesson 1) you're just fitting noise. Reseed a few times: the "best" lookback jumps around and the OOS numbers swing wildly. A result that won't sit still isn't an edge.

The capstone makes the out-of-sample test real: beat buy-and-hold on data the strategy never saw.
✅ You can now say…
  • Total return is the headline; it hides how you got there.
  • Sharpe = mean daily return ÷ its std × √252 — reward per unit of wobble (>1 is decent).
  • Max drawdown is the worst peak-to-trough fall — the pain that decides if you'd have held on.
  • In-sample = data you tuned on; out-of-sample = unseen data you judge on.
  • Tuning to look great in-sample is overfitting; only the out-of-sample score is honest.
