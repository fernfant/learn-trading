Lesson 15 · A limit order rests — price-time priority

A market order shouted "fill me now!" (Lesson 14). A limit order is patient: "fill me only at my price or better — otherwise wait." If it can't trade right away it rests and joins a queue. The exchange serves that queue by price-time priority: better price first; among equal prices, first-in-first-out. Your place in line decides whether you actually get filled.

🪜 The queue at a price level

These are buyers (bids) resting at a price, waiting for a seller. Join the queue at the going price and you fall in behind everyone already there. Pay a better price and you jump to the very front. Then send in a market sell — it eats the front of the line first.

💡 Two rules, in order. Price first: a higher bid always beats a lower one. Time second: among orders at the same price, whoever arrived earlier sits ahead. Arrival is tracked with a plain sequence number (#1, #2, #3…), not a clock — two orders can never share one, so there are no ties.
Your place in line is decided the instant you post. Join late at the same price as the crowd and a market order may run out before it reaches you — you wait, untouched, while the people ahead of you trade. To get filled sooner you must pay a better price to jump the queue, or arrive earlier. This is why HFTs fight over microseconds (Lesson 20): a better spot in line is the difference between trading and waiting.
✅ You can now say…
  • A limit order fills only at its price or better; if it can't, it rests in the book.
  • Resting orders are served by price-time priority: better price first, then earliest arrival (FIFO).
  • Arrival order is a sequence number, not a wall-clock time.
  • A market order consumes from the front of the queue — your place in line decides if you trade.
