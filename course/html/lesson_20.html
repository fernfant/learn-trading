Lesson 20 · Latency & the queue — why microseconds pay

Price-time priority (Lesson 15) says best price wins, and at the same price whoever arrived first sits at the front of the queue and fills first. So when two market makers quote the identical price, the trade goes to whoever's order got there first — and that's decided by latency. Lower latency ⇒ earlier in line ⇒ more fills ⇒ more spread captured. Speed is money.

💡 It's the same four-cent spread either way. Both makers quote fair ± 0.05 — identical prices, identical edge per fill. The only thing that differs is who gets to the front of the queue first. Halve your latency and you roughly double your share of the flow. That curve is the entire business case for co-location (a rack inside the exchange) and microwave links (light through air beats light through glass) — the story in Michael Lewis's Flash Boys.
Speed cuts both ways — and it isn't free. A fast maker also cancels stale quotes before a price move runs them over; the slow maker is still resting there and gets picked off (a preview of adverse selection, Lesson 21). But the speed itself costs a fortune — racks, fibre, microwave towers — and when everyone is fast, nobody is. The spread is still the only edge; latency just decides whose hand it lands in. No free money here either.
✅ You can now say…
  • At the same price, price-time priority hands the fill to whoever's order arrived first — i.e. to lower latency.
  • A maker's fill share rises as its latency falls (≈ proportional to 1 / latency); halving latency roughly doubles the flow you win.
  • Speed wins more good flow and dodges more bad flow by cancelling faster — both edges point the same way: faster is richer.
  • This is co-location and the microsecond arms race (Flash Boys); in ../exchange.py it's the line share = speed/(speed+rival).
