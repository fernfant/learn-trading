Lesson 19 · Inventory risk — skew your quotes

As market maker you only wanted the spread. But every fill leaves you holding stock you didn't want — a customer buys, so you're short; a customer sells, so you're long. That leftover inventory is directional risk. The fix: skew your quotes by inventory. Quote around reservation = fair − k·inventory, not the raw mid — lean your prices toward whichever side gets you back to flat.

📊 The inventory desk — lean your quotes to stay flat

Run the same customer flow with skew k on. At k = 0 inventory random-walks and balloons (naked risk). Slide k up and watch the quotes lean, the gauge snap back toward center, and the inventory line hug zero.

💡 The quotes lean toward flat. Go long and the reservation price drops below fair, so both quotes shift down: a cheap ask begs someone to take stock off you, a stingy bid stops you buying more. Go short and it flips up. You never predict the price — you just price yourself back toward zero. That's the Avellaneda–Stoikov reservation price: fair − k·inventory.
It's not free. Leaning your quotes means shading them away from the mid, so you capture a little less spread per trade. Crank k too high and you give away edge for inventory control you don't need. The job (Lesson 22) is to find the sweet spot: capture the spread AND stay flat.
✅ You can now say…
  • Every fill leaves the maker holding inventory — a position, and so directional risk.
  • With no skew, inventory random-walks away from zero and balloons; one move wipes out the spread you earned.
  • The fix: quote around a reservation price fair − k·inventory, leaning toward flat.
  • More skew = tighter inventory but slightly less spread captured — the Avellaneda–Stoikov trade-off.
