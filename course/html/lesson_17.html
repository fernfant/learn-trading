Lesson 17 · Market data & the tape — what an HFT actually sees

You built the matching engine. Now: what does a trader actually look at? Two feeds stream out of the exchange. The book feed (the depth) is a snapshot of resting liquidity — best bid & ask plus the sizes stacked behind them. The trade feed (the tape) is the running list of trades that actually executed, each tagged with the aggressor. The book is a photograph of intentions; the tape is a recording of events.

Resting liquidity is set; nothing has traded yet — the tape is empty.
💡 The aggressor tag is the gold on the tape. Every print is colored by who crossed the spread to make it happen — green = a buyer lifted the ask, red = a seller hit the bid. Tally a run of green prints and you've inferred buying pressure — nobody labeled it for you. That inference, plus the live depth imbalance, is the raw input to every trading decision.
Depth is intentions, not promises. Resting orders on the ladder can be cancelled in a microsecond — a fat bid you're leaning on may vanish the instant real selling arrives. The tape, by contrast, is trades that already happened: it can't be un-printed. When the two disagree (deep book, but the tape keeps trading through it), trust the tape.
✅ You can now say…
  • An exchange publishes two feeds: the book / depth (resting liquidity, a snapshot) and the tape (executed trades, a stream).
  • Top-of-book = best bid & ask + their sizes; depth = the sizes stacked at each level behind them.
  • Each tape print carries an aggressor side — the impatient party who crossed the spread. Tallying it lets you read buying vs selling pressure.
  • Derived signals: mid = (bid+ask)/2 and imbalance = bid_size/(bid_size+ask_size) — the first numbers any trader's eye reads.
  • In exchange.py these are snapshot() and the tape — the window every later market-maker watches.
