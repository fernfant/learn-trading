Lesson 13 · The order book — the book is the market

An exchange keeps almost nothing: just two sorted lists. BIDS are buyers, sorted highest-price-first (the most anyone will pay). ASKS are sellers, sorted lowest-price-first (the least anyone will accept). The top of each is the best bid / best ask; the gap is the spread; their average is the mid — and that mid is exactly the single "price" Track 1 traded against.

💡 The mid is where Track 1's price lived. You can never actually trade at the mid — buying costs you the best ask, selling gets you the best bid. But it's the fair reference halfway between, and it's the single number market.py handed you as "the price" all along.
Try posting a bid above the best ask (e.g. a bid at 100.10). That buyer would pay more than the cheapest seller asks — they should trade instantly. This toy book just stacks it and the spread goes negative (a "crossed" book), which a real exchange never allows. Turning two willing orders into a trade is the matching engine — Lessons 14 and 16.
✅ You can now say…
  • An order book is two sorted lists: bids (buyers, high→low) and asks (sellers, low→high).
  • The tops are the best bid and best ask; the gap is the spread; the average is the mid.
  • The mid is exactly the single "price" Track 1's market.py traded against.
  • Depth = size resting at each level; a better quote tightens the spread.
  • A book is never crossed — a bid at or above the best ask must trade (next lessons).
